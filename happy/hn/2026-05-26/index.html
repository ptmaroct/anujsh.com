Pope Leo XIV publishes Magnifica Humanitas, a 40-page encyclical on AI and the dignity of work.

The Vatican has done what every major AI lab has avoided: written down what it will not trade.

Magnifica Humanitas is the first papal encyclical written explicitly with synthetic intelligence in mind. It does not condemn AI, and it does not endorse it. What it does is set out red lines: machines must not adjudicate human worth, displace the worker without recourse, mediate the sacraments, or be permitted to wage war autonomously. The argument is grounded less in scripture than in 19th-century labour theology — Rerum Novarum updated for an era where the loom is a transformer.

Whatever you think of the institution, the document is unusually concrete for a moral text. It calls on Catholic universities, hospitals and dioceses to publish AI usage policies, and on Catholic-affiliated investors to apply a 'discernment screen' to AI portfolios. Expect long quoting in policy papers, court filings and union briefs for years. The HN thread is a fascinating split — sceptics, theologians, people who work at frontier labs, and a Vatican press officer answering questions in real time.

    California moves to exempt Linux from its age-verification law after FOSS backlash.

    The lawmaker who wrote the original bill has now proposed the amendment carving out general-purpose operating systems where no single vendor controls distribution. In practice that means Linux, BSDs and most niche OSes are exempt; Windows, macOS, iOS and Android are not. The bill still has real teeth for the big platforms — but the precedent that age-gating cannot be applied to community-built software is, on its own, a structural win worth a year of lobbying.

    Microsoft Copilot Cowork can be coaxed into silently exfiltrating files from a tenant.

    A clean writeup of a real prompt-injection chain: a poisoned shared document instructs Copilot Cowork to summarise unrelated tenant files into an external URL it controls. No user click required, no obvious audit trail. Microsoft has shipped a partial mitigation but the underlying problem — that 'agents' inherit user permissions wholesale — is not fixable by patch. If you have rolled out Copilot Cowork, this is the week to lock down outbound network policy for the agent.

    Netherlands seizes 800 servers and arrests two over a long-running bulletproof hoster.

    Dutch police, with Europol, have taken down what investigators call one of the largest cybercrime infrastructure providers in Europe. The hoster knowingly served ransomware C2, info-stealer panels and child-abuse forums for years. The interesting bit is the legal theory: the two operators are being charged not just with hosting but with aiding the underlying attacks — a precedent that, if it sticks, reshapes the legal exposure of every grey-market hoster on the continent.

    Mullvad begins rolling out a new exit-IP mitigation across its VPN fleet.

    The new scheme rotates exit IPs across customers more aggressively, breaking the long-standing trick of fingerprinting a single Mullvad user via a sticky outbound address. The post is unusually candid about the tradeoffs — yes, a small minority of services will misbehave when the IP changes mid-session, and Mullvad documents which ones. The kind of careful, boring engineering choice that quietly raises the floor for everyone else.

    A fundamental principle of aeronautical engineering has been overturned.

    Decades-old textbooks describe lift generation in a way that, it turns out, gets the wing-airflow coupling subtly but consistently wrong. A new joint paper from NASA Langley and DLR reproduces the experiment with modern instrumentation and proposes a revised model — one that, among other things, predicts drag at transonic speeds about 4% more accurately. Practical impact is marginal for existing fleets but non-trivial for next-generation supersonic and high-altitude designs.

    Norway buys two petabytes of Huawei flash storage to anchor a national LLM training cluster.

    A sovereign-AI procurement that quietly punches a hole in the Western consensus on Huawei. Oslo's reasoning, per the article, is pragmatic: Western flash vendors could not meet the timeline at the price. The kit will sit behind a public-money research cluster training Norwegian-language foundation models. Expect a noisy diplomatic back-and-forth and, more interestingly, more European governments testing exactly how firm the no-Huawei line is for non-telecoms gear.

    IBM spins off the first pure-play quantum chip foundry, backed by $2B in CHIPS Act money.

    The new entity will run IBM's 300mm superconducting silicon line as a contract manufacturer for quantum-chip designers — a structural bet that quantum will go fabless before it goes mainstream. It is also a quiet acknowledgment that IBM itself is no longer guaranteed to win the qubit-architecture race, and would rather collect rent on the fab than place every chip. Whether anyone actually buys reticles is the next question.
