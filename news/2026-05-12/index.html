Hardware Attestation as Monopoly Enabler

Attestation isn't a security primitive — it's permission to keep existing as a platform.

GrapheneOS lays out, calmly and at length, what most of us have been muttering for two years: hardware attestation, sold to developers as a fraud-prevention feature, has quietly become the lever that lets Apple, Google and a small club of payment networks decide which operating systems are allowed to exist on consumer hardware. The argument lands because it's specific — banking apps refuse to launch on de-Googled phones, ride-hailing apps gate on Play Integrity, even chat clients are starting to consult the chip. The same primitive that's supposed to protect you also conveniently locks you out of running anything outside the sanctioned stack.

What makes this worth your Friday and not just your Tuesday is the regulatory subtext. The EU's DMA already treats app store gatekeeping as a competition problem; attestation is the next obvious frontier, and the post reads like a prepared brief for whoever gets the case. If you ship anything that depends on an open ecosystem — alternative browsers, sideloaded apps, a custom ROM, even a niche Linux phone — this is the fight that decides whether you have a market in five years.
