A Texas woman was arrested for a Facebook post questioning her town's water quality.

The moment a municipality treats a citizen's complaint about brown tap water as a criminal matter, you have stopped governing and started managing optics.

The facts are uncontested: a resident of a small Texas town posted on a neighbourhood Facebook group that the tap water looked, smelled and tasted off; police, citing a state 'misinformation' statute that local prosecutors had until now treated as ornamental, showed up at her door and booked her. The water utility has since said its tests are normal — which is, of course, the whole problem being argued about. The HN thread is unusually focused: lawyers walking through the actual statute, public-health folks pointing out that 'looks wrong' is the canonical first signal of a contamination event, and a long sub-thread on what changed in the last year that made this charge thinkable.

The story is going to outrun its initial reporting. Either the test data hold up and this becomes a textbook case study in chilling lawful speech, or they don't and it becomes something rather worse for the municipality. Either way, every city attorney watching is going to pull their own version of this ordinance off the shelf and read it again — and that is the part with the longest tail.

    SpaceX flies Starship v3 — first integrated test of the redesigned upper stage.

    The headline number is a successful ascent, controlled re-entry and a soft splashdown of both stages; the real news is in the smaller numbers. V3 is meaningfully shorter, uses an upgraded Raptor 3 with fewer welds, and is the first variant intended from the start to carry the Mars-architecture life-support stack. SpaceX still has to demonstrate orbital refuel and full reuse, but on yesterday's evidence the development tempo isn't slowing — which, depending on your priors, is either impressive engineering discipline or a regulatory problem the FAA is about to inherit again.

    Oura confirms it receives government demands for user data — won't say how many.

    The ring-maker's transparency-page update concedes what privacy researchers had been assuming for a year: yes, law enforcement asks for sleep, heart-rate and location data; no, the company isn't publishing counts, geographies, or how often it complies. The post is studiously vague where vagueness is the point. The thread quickly converges on the obvious: a wearable that knows when you're asleep and where you slept is an attractive subpoena target, and the absence of a real transparency report is the report.

    sp.h — one developer's attempt to give C the standard library it never had.

    A single header (well, several headers behind one) providing strings that know their length, dynamic arrays, hash maps, arena allocators, optionals, and a unit-test harness — all in portable C99, all without depending on libc beyond `malloc`. There are plenty of 'fix C' libraries; this one is unusual in being aggressively boring, honest about its scope, and small enough to vendor in one commit. Expect a long debate in the comments and quiet adoption in the side projects of the same people doing the debating.

    .NET 11 finally lands union types in C# — and the design is sharper than the wait suggested.

    Andrew Lock walks through the preview-2 implementation of discriminated unions, which arrive with exhaustive pattern matching, record-like syntax, and source-generated boilerplate that doesn't look like Java. The interesting bit is the interop: existing nullable-reference and tuple machinery have been quietly retrofitted to play with unions, so the migration path for legacy code is genuinely incremental. After fifteen years of asking, the C# team appears to have shipped a thing worth waiting for.

    Someone has disassembled the 80386's microcode — every last word of it.

    A multi-year reverse-engineering project that started with die shots and ends with annotated, reconstructed microcode for Intel's first 32-bit chip. The writeup is dense in the best way — it makes the difference between hardwired and microcoded instructions tangible, names the helper routines, and traces a few of the more arcane prefixed-instruction paths that nobody seems to have published before. CPU-archaeology is having a quietly extraordinary decade.

    On the <dl>: a love letter to HTML's most misunderstood list.

    Ben Myers makes the case for the description list — long abused as a 'two-column layout' hack — as one of the most semantically rich elements in HTML, and shows what screen readers actually do with it. The piece is from 2021 but resurfaced this week with new energy, partly because the latest accessibility tooling now exposes the term/definition relationship in ways that finally reward authors who used it correctly. A small win for the people who write their HTML by hand.

    We made our filesystem 47× faster by deleting it.

    The Microsandbox team realised their OCI image layer was paying for a filesystem they were re-creating on every container start, and replaced it with a content-addressed mmap-backed blob the kernel could fault in on demand. The post is short, the benchmark methodology is explicit, and the lesson is the kind senior engineers nod along to: the fastest code is the code you didn't write because you noticed you didn't need it.
