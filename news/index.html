Using AI to write better code more slowly

The honest measurement is throughput per unit of understanding, not lines per hour.

Nolan Lawson's essay is the rare grown-up take on AI coding assistants — neither hype nor allergy. He argues that the productivity story is upside-down: the assistant is most useful when you slow down, treat its output as a draft, and read every line you accept. That sounds boring; it is also the only way to keep ownership of a codebase that the model will not be paged on at 3 a.m.

What gives it teeth is the embedded admission that this is harder than the alternative. It is easier to accept the diff, ship, and move on; the bill arrives later, in incident reviews. The essay is going to outlive the news cycle because every team is currently arguing some version of this in standup.

    A sleep-like consolidation mechanism for LLMs

    A new arXiv paper proposes interleaving training with offline 'consolidation' phases that replay condensed memories — explicitly inspired by mammalian sleep. The reported gains on continual-learning benchmarks are modest but the framing is the interesting part: instead of one monolithic train run, models get periodic, cheap nights. If it generalises, this changes how labs schedule compute.
