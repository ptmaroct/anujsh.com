The dead economy theory

If the headline indicators are healthy and the lived experience isn't, the indicators are wrong — or measuring a different economy from the one you live in.

McGrann's central claim is unpleasantly tidy: the U.S. economy is technically alive — GDP is fine, equities are fine, unemployment is fine — and yet the median household's experience of work, housing, healthcare, and the social contract has degraded in ways the aggregate numbers refuse to register. He argues this isn't a paradox; it's the predictable end-state of an economy whose growth has concentrated upward for long enough that the dashboard and the dining table have decoupled.

Whether or not you buy the framing, the essay is the kind of writing HN rewards when it shows up: clear thesis, real numbers, no policy snake-oil at the end. Worth reading twice — once for the argument, once to ask which of your own working assumptions about 'the economy' are downstream of a dashboard that may not be measuring what you think it measures. The 862-comment thread is half people violently agreeing and half people doing the exact behaviour the essay describes.

    Blue Origin's New Glenn blows up during static fire test

    A scheduled hold-down test of the second-stage engines turned into a full RUD on the pad, with vehicle and a meaningful chunk of test infrastructure lost. No injuries reported. Early footage suggests an oxidiser-side failure, but the company's only official line so far is 'anomaly under investigation'.

    The optics are bad — New Glenn was finally building a flight cadence after years of delays — and the schedule pain is real, because rebuilding the test stand is on the critical path for the next two contracted launches. SpaceX will keep their grin private; the rest of the industry should not, because a single-provider heavy-lift market is good for nobody.

    GTA 6 developers unionize

    Rockstar's North American development staff have voted to form a union — the first wall-to-wall recognition at a tier-1 game studio in the U.S. The vote follows years of organising rounds at smaller studios (Activision QA, ZeniMax, Sega) that suddenly look, in hindsight, like the on-ramp to exactly this.

    Rockstar is, by revenue and cultural footprint, the closest thing the game industry has to a flagship. A union here changes the negotiating baseline for every studio that ships a AAA title, not just for crunch but for IP, severance, and AI-tooling clauses. Watch what Take-Two does next; the rest of the industry will copy that response.

    Liquid AI reveals 8B-A1B MoE trained on 38T tokens

    Liquid's new release is an 8-billion-parameter mixture-of-experts model with one billion active at inference, trained on a genuinely large 38-trillion-token corpus. Benchmarks place it within striking distance of much larger dense models on reasoning and coding tasks, and well ahead on inference cost-per-token.

    The interesting part is not the leaderboard — it's the trajectory. Sparse activation plus aggressive data scaling is increasingly the route to 'good enough at a fraction of the cost', and it is starting to look like the default architecture for the next generation of self-hosted, mid-tier models.

    Volkswagen blocks Home Assistant by requiring client assertion

    Volkswagen has rolled out a server-side change that demands a signed client assertion from any client talking to its car API, effectively shutting out the Home Assistant integration that thousands of VW/Audi owners use to read state and control their own vehicles. There is no documented developer path to get a legitimate assertion.

    It is the same playbook automakers keep running: collect telemetry from the customer, lock the customer out of their own data, then sell the data back as a subscription. Right-to-repair and 'right-to-read' arguments converge here, and this is the kind of case lawmakers will actually understand because the affected users are loud, organised, and on GitHub.

    Is AI causing a repeat of frontend's lost decade?

    The thesis: frontend already lived through one decade where churn (Angular → React → Vue → Svelte, then framework-of-the-week) outran any real productivity gain, and the LLM-powered scaffolding boom is doing it again — only this time the throwaway code is being generated faster than the team can read it, let alone maintain it.

    Reasonable people will disagree on whether AI is making this worse or better, but the diagnostic checklist in the post is sharp: are you choosing tools you can debug, generating code you can read, and producing systems someone other than the original author can maintain? Those questions did not get less important when the autocomplete got smarter.

    Citing 'severe' math deficits, UC faculty demand a return to SAT tests for STEM

    Four years after the University of California system dropped standardised testing entirely, a coalition of faculty in math-heavy departments are asking for the SAT back — not as a magic predictor, but as a triage signal for which incoming students need remediation before they hit calculus. They are pointing to internal placement data showing a sharp rise in students unable to clear pre-calc requirements.

    The politics here are radioactive — the original test-optional move was made partly on equity grounds and partly under legal pressure — but the underlying empirical question is fair: when you remove a signal, what fills the void? At UC's scale, 'nothing' is not a sustainable answer.
